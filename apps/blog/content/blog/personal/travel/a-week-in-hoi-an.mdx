I arrived on a Tuesday in May, which is apparently the worst time to visit — too hot, the guide said. But the morning light at 6 AM, when the riverside streets are still empty and a fine mist hangs over the Thu Bồn, is the kind of thing I would travel badly-timed for every time.

![Empty riverside streets at dawn in Hội An](/images/personal/hoi-an-dawn.svg)

## The Old Town at Dawn

Hội An's Ancient Town is a UNESCO World Heritage site, which means it's on every itinerary and everyone is slightly protective of it in a way that makes you feel guilty for being a tourist. The lanterns strung between buildings, the yellow-painted walls, the narrow shop-fronts — you've seen it in photographs.

What you haven't seen in photographs is the woman who sets up her bánh mì cart at the same spot every morning at 5:30. Thirty thousand dong. She wraps it in newspaper. She doesn't speak English and I don't speak Vietnamese, but she understood *"extra chilli"* from the way I pointed, and we understood each other perfectly.

## The Tailor

Everyone warns you about the Hội An tailors. *They'll rush it. The quality won't last. You'll regret it.* Some of that is true. But I found a small shop on a side street run by a woman who had been there for thirty years, and she made me a linen shirt in two days that I've worn more than anything I own.

We spent an hour talking about fabric. She showed me how the weight of a linen changes how it falls. She was not trying to sell me anything in that hour. She was just telling me something she knew that she thought I should know.

I think about that a lot — how expertise becomes generosity when it's given freely.

## The Shoe Man

On my last morning, I stepped on a nail somewhere near the Japanese Covered Bridge. Not badly — just enough to pull the sole of my sandal loose.

An elderly man was sitting on a low stool twenty meters away, repairing shoes with a small kit in a wooden box. He looked at my sandal for about two seconds, gestured for me to sit on the curb beside him, and fixed it in four minutes. He charged me ten thousand dong — about forty cents — and waved off the extra I tried to offer.

I walked back to my hotel thinking: this is the thing travel keeps trying to teach me. Not the monuments. Not the food (though the food). The ten-thousand-dong moments, where someone does something small and skilled and completely without fuss, and you walk away slightly adjusted.

![Lanterns reflected on the river at night](/images/personal/hoi-an-lanterns.svg)

## What I Brought Home

A linen shirt. A scar on my heel that's faded now. The memory of lanterns reflected in the river at 9 PM when the town finally goes soft and quiet. And the habit, since then, of taking the side streets.
