I have a friend who has been building a boat for eleven years.

Not a small boat. A 28-foot wooden sailboat, hand-planked, in his garage. He works on it on weekend mornings, sometimes for two hours, sometimes for twenty minutes before the kids wake up. He has no launch date. He may never launch it. He doesn't seem concerned either way.

I asked him once if it bothered him — working on something for so long without finishing. He looked at me like I'd asked something slightly strange.

*"I'm not trying to finish it,"* he said. *"I'm making a boat."*

![A half-finished boat in a garage workshop](/images/personal/boat-workshop.svg)

---

I've been thinking about that distinction a lot while working on this blog.

The pressure to ship is real and, often, useful. Deadlines create clarity. Constraints make decisions. There's genuine virtue in done over perfect. I believe all of that.

But there's another kind of work — the kind that teaches you something new every session precisely because it's not finished. The boat that teaches you about wood grain. The essay that keeps revealing what you actually think. The side project that, if you're honest, you'd be a little sad to call complete.

---

I started this blog with a list of things I wanted to write about. The list is still largely unwritten. What I've discovered, post by post, is that the list was wrong — not because the topics aren't interesting, but because I didn't know yet what I actually wanted to say about them.

Writing is how I find out.

The boat builder knows something about this. You don't build the boat to have the boat. You build the boat to learn how to build the boat. The object that comes out the other end is almost incidental — it's proof that the learning happened, evidence of the hours.

---

I'm not sure this is a philosophy you can apply universally. Some things *should* be finished, and quickly. But for the personal projects — the ones that don't have a customer, a deadline, a revenue goal — I wonder if we'd build better things, and enjoy building them more, if we stopped treating incompleteness as a problem to solve.

The boat sits in my friend's garage, half-planked and beautiful.

He goes in every Saturday morning.

That's enough.
